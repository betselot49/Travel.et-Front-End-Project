History

Adwa

The Battle of Adwa was fought on March 1, 1896 between Ethiopia and Italy near the town of Adwa in northern Ethiopia. The Ethiopians were victorious, decisively defeating the Italian forces. This victory marked an important milestone in Ethiopian success against European colonialism, becoming a symbol of African pride and a source of inspiration to African independence movements across the continent. Ethiopia was thereby one of the only African nations to effectively resist European colonization efforts throughout the 19th century and into the 20th century. Moreover, it showed that Africa could triumph over European invaders with courage and resourcefulness rather than only by relying on superior military strength or arms. This victory is still remembered today as a sign of Ethiopian strength and national pride.

Origin of humanity

Ethiopia is one of the earliest known centers of human civilization and holds special place in human history. It is believed by some to be the original source of humanity. This is supported by some science findings, including the world-renowned discovery of the 3 million-year-old fossil, "Lucy," in Ethiopia's Rift Valley in 1974. Lucy represents one of our earliest ancestors who had a mix between ape and human characteristics. The find showed that our early ancestors had an upright posture, similar to humans today, marking an important milestone in our evolution. Ethiopia has also been home to grand civilizations like the Aksumite Empire which played a major role in ancient trade relations with Greece, Rome and Egypt and established Christianity as a state religion which later spread throughout North Africa and Europe in the fourth century A.D.

Ancient Civilisation

Ethiopian ancient civilization has a long and storied history, dating back to at least the 10th century BC. Uniquely for its region, Ethiopia was never colonized and proud of its regional autonomy. In medieval times, political and spiritual power were strongly linked, centered around the powerful Ethiopian Orthodox Church. Architecture from this period is a mix of native Aksumite styles, mixed with influences from Christianity, South Arabia, Greece and Rome – notably stelae monuments in Northern Ethiopia. The golden age ranged from 1270-1533 CE before Falasha Jews completed their exodus. By 1600s the country ascended within Africa as an important geopolitical power under emperor Menelik II who famously defeated Italian forces in 1896 at the Battle of Adowa. Ethiopia is home to many important cultural sites including Lalibela Rock-Hewn Churches, Axum Stele Fields and Gondar Castles testament to a vibrant past along far flung imperial roads!

Culture

The Ethiopian coffee origin story dates back to over 1000 years. It is believed that the indigenous Oromo tribe was the first to recognize the energizing effects of the beans found growing wild near their homeland of Kaffa in South-West Ethiopia.

Traditional: Ancient rituals, colorful dress, feast, exchanging of gifts, events. Church: Blessing, prayer, exchange vows, readings, hymns, sharing bread/wine.

Ethiopian holidays are colourful and joyous. Traditional dances , music, songs, traditional clothing and great food all make for an unforgettable experience.

Ethiopian traditional clothing is unique and vibrant. It includes loose, white robes for men known as Gabi and brightly coloured wrap dresses for women known as Habesha kemis decorated with intricate Ethiopian crosses, scarfs, jewellery and hats.

Art and Sience in Ethiopia

The National Museum of Ethiopia in Addis Ababa is renowned for its collection of art and science specimens. It houses a wide range of artifacts, sculptures and archaeological finds dating back centuries. Its holdings include Paleolithic and Mesolithic stone tools, traditional masks, ceramics and manuscripts, as well as more modern scientific instruments in the form of geological specimens, animal dioramas and ancient coins. Visitors can explore the history of the country through interactive displays, hands-on workshops and guided tours.

Music

Ethiopian music is multi-cultural, featuring traditional rhythms and instruments like azmari, kebero and masenqo. Popular legends include Aster Aweke, Mahmoud Ahmed and Mulatu Astatke.
